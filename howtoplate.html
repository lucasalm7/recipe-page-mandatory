create height

“It's important that you don't separate the food trying to fill the plate - build from the bottom up,” he says. “If you could use a little structure, you can always purchase a ring mould to start with a base. Simply pack some food in the mould and gently lift it up. Now you have a base to build on.”

Other ideas for creating height on the plate include:

  • Using vibrant leaves as a bed for salads. Try incorporating different sizes and colours of salad leaves, like radicchio, little gem, and frisée.
  • Rather than serving ice cream and sorbet on the side, place a quenelle on top of the dessert, as demonstrated by London restaurant, Casa Fofò.

cut meat horizontally

“Slice your meat on a 45-degree bias, and also slice against the grain of the meat for a more tender cut,” he says. “Show off that perfect medium-rare steak!”

This presentation technique works equally well with fish and vegetables, too. Here are a few ways to present other ingredients horizontally:

  • When serving crudo, fan out wafer-thin pieces of tuna, salmon, and sea bass across the base of a plate.
  • This same fanning technique can be adapted for aubergines, asparagus, carrots and cauliflower. Overlap grilled asparagus on the base of a plate and top with other ingredients, or slice cauliflower, pan-fry and place each piece next to one another.

play with textures

“I like to play up contrasting textures on my plate,” she adds. “Foams are usually really helpful in plating and can be really easily done, playing around with different sauces and textures.”

Here are some ideas of how to incorporate sauces and foams in your dish:

  • Do like Llewellyn's do and use a vibrant vichyssoise as a base for fish and meat.
  • Take inspiration from Berber and Q and serve sweet sauces on the side of dessert, so customers can enjoy as much or as little as they like.

play with textures

"Don't be afraid to let colours clash a little — contrast is what gives a plate life," he says. "If everything looks the same shade, the dish loses depth. Aim for balance: let bright ingredients pop against neutral tones."

Other ideas for creating colour contrast on the plate include:

  • Pairing opposites: Combine warm tones like orange or red with cool greens or purples to make both stand out.
  • Using neutral backgrounds: White plates or pale sauces can help highlight vibrant ingredients without overwhelming the eye.
  • Adding a finishing touch: A sprinkle of herbs, edible flowers, or a drizzle of dark reduction can create a visual focal point that ties the dish together.

choose the plates

"The plate is your canvas — it should complement, not compete with, the food," he says. "Think about how the shape, size, and colour of the plate can enhance what you're serving. A well-chosen plate frames the dish and guides the eye."

Other ideas for choosing the right plates include:

  • Consider the portion and style: Smaller plates create intimacy for refined dishes, while wide, flat plates give space for artistic layouts.
  • Match colour to purpose: Dark plates can make bright ingredients glow, while white ones keep the focus on the food's natural beauty.
  • Think about texture and finish: Matte plates offer sophistication and photograph well; glossy ones add shine and energy to vibrant dishes.

serve smaller portions

"Less can be more when it comes to plating," he says. "Smaller portions give every element room to breathe, allowing the diner to appreciate texture, colour, and balance. It's about creating focus, not abundance."

Other ideas for refining portion size include:

  • Prioritize quality over quantity: A few perfectly cooked components are more impressive than a crowded plate.
  • Use negative space intentionally: Empty areas draw the eye to the centre of the dish and make the presentation look deliberate and elegant.
  • Think about the diner's journey: Smaller servings invite curiosity and leave room for the next course — enhancing the overall dining experience.
